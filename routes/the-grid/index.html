THE GRID

  • from the perspective of a new
  • graphic designer and learner

    • Entry by Vincent Liu, 4/19/2022
    with credit to Negotiating Ungers

    The grid is used as a framework for design. You don’t have to strictly follow the grid for everything.

    It was interesting to discover that most designs have an underlying structure to them.

    When first discovering the grid in class, it felt very safe and easy to use.

    As long as everything aligned on one of the grid lines, the design was going to work. But the grid is also limiting, acting as a constraint.

    When you starting trying to fill all the white spaces within the grid, that’s when it starts to lose its magic.

    Breaking the Grid

    All of your work is essentially squished into this large rectangle.

    All of the ideas and forms are governed by a larger system.

    The grid is helpful to use when starting a new design, to act as an outline when there is none. However, overly relying on the grid can make your designs very rigid.

    Everything will be confined into small blocks.

    There is a lack of movement or motion.

    Everything is TOO perfect.
    It’s nice to find a balance between the two. For example, overlaying forms or typography can help to distort the grid.

    The grid should be more of a suggestion, rather than the end all be all. Nothing should get between a maker’s creative decisions and the execution of it.

    That intuitive gut feeling is a designer’s most valuable asset, good things always come out of following it.

    Allow the grid to give structure to your work, but do not let it control how your work will turn out.


    Putting it into practice in my own work:

    Some of my own RULES regarding the GRID


    1. Don’t detest the grid, it’s useful.

    2. Don’t rely on the grid too much.

    3. Feel safe while working with the grid.

    4. Break the grid when needed, it’s quite brittle.

    5. Follow that gut feeling.

    6. Your work should always be YOUR work.

    7. Allow the grid to give structure to your work, but do not let it control how your work will turn out.
